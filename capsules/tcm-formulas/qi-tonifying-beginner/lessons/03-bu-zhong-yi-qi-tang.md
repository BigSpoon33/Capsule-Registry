# Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang (Supplement the Middle and Augment the Qi Decoction)
*Source: Chen & Chen p.581 | Lesson 3 of 4*

---

## Pattern
**Spleen and stomach qi deficiency with qi sinking**

---

## Clinical Manifestations

- Chronic Fatigue
- Chronic Diarrhea
- Dizziness
- Poor Appetite
- Prolapse
- Shortness of Breath
- Weak Voice

**Tongue:** Pale with thin white coating
**Pulse:** Weak, especially in the middle position, may be thready

---

## Composition

| Herb (Pin Yin) | Pharmaceutical Name | Dosage | Role |
|----------------|---------------------|--------|------|
| Huang Qi | Radix Astragali | 15-30g | Chief |
| Ren Shen | Radix et Rhizoma Ginseng | 9-15g | Chief |
| Bai Zhu | Rhizoma Atractylodis Macrocephalae | 9-15g | Deputy |
| Gan Cao | Radix et Rhizoma Glycyrrhizae | 6-9g | Deputy |
| Dang Gui | Radix Angelicae Sinensis | 6-12g | Assistant |
| Chen Pi | Pericarpium Citri Reticulatae | 6-9g | Assistant |
| Sheng Ma | Rhizoma Cimicifugae | 3-6g | Envoy |
| Chai Hu | Radix Bupleuri | 3-6g | Envoy |

---

## Formula Analysis

**Chief (君):** Huang Qi & Ren Shen

**Deputy (臣):** Bai Zhu & Gan Cao

**Assistant (佐):** Dang Gui & Chen Pi

**Envoy (使):** Sheng Ma & Chai Hu

---

## Actions & Indications

- Tonify middle jiao qi
- Raise yang qi
- Lift the clear

---

## Clinical Background

Master formula for "middle qi sinking" - the concept that spleen qi becomes too weak to hold organs in place. Represents the perfect balance of tonifying without stagnation due to lifting herbs.

---

## Common Modifications

| Condition | Add Herbs | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Severe prolapse | Sheng Ma + Ma Huang Gen | Stronger lifting action |
| With blood deficiency | He Shou Wu + E Jiao | Enhance blood nourishing |
| Poor digestion | Sha Ren + Mu Xiang | Promote qi movement |
| With dampness | Cang Zhu + Hou Po | Transform dampness |

---

## Distinguishing Features

Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang is the **lifting formula**. It shares the Si Jun Zi Tang base but adds Huang Qi (the largest dose, 15-30g) as co-chief, Dang Gui to nourish blood, Chen Pi to prevent stagnation, and crucially **Sheng Ma + Chai Hu** to raise sinking yang qi.

Key distinguishing signs:
- **Organ prolapse** (uterus, rectum, stomach, bladder) is the hallmark indication
- Symptoms worsen after meals or prolonged standing
- Chronic fatigue that improves with rest, shortness of breath on exertion

---

## Exam Tips (CALE/NCCAOM)

- **Chief herb(s) to memorize:** Huang Qi (Radix Astragali), Ren Shen (Radix et Rhizoma Ginseng)
- **Classic symptom cluster:** Chronic Fatigue, Chronic Diarrhea, Dizziness, Poor Appetite
- **Tongue/Pulse:** Pale with thin white coating / Weak, especially in the middle position, may be thready
- Key indication: prolapse of organs
- Distinguished from Si Jun Zi Tang by addition of lifting herbs
- Pulse will be weak in middle position (stomach/spleen)

---

## Related Formulas

- **Si Jun Zi Tang**: foundational spleen qi tonic, no dampness-resolving or lifting herbs
- **Liu Jun Zi Tang**: adds Chen Pi + Ban Xia for concurrent phlegm-dampness
- **Sheng Mai San**: targets qi-yin deficiency with sweating, only 3 herbs
