## 1. Acute Rebellious Stomach Qi vs. Liver Stagnation

**Question:**
A patient presents with sudden, violent hiccoughing, nausea, and a sensation of fullness in the epigastrium. They report a strong desire to vomit but are unable to do so, accompanied by a feeling of coldness in the stomach. The tongue coating is white and greasy, and the pulse is deep and wiry. How would you differentiate this presentation from hiccough caused by Liver Qi stagnation, and how does Ding Xiang address the specific pathomechanism of cold rebellion in this case?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- Ding Xiang's ability to enter the Spleen and Stomach channels to warm the middle burner and direct rebellious Stomach qi downward.
- The distinction between hiccough caused by cold deficiency (treated by Ding Xiang) versus hiccough caused by Liver Qi stagnation rising.
- The role of Ding Xiang's aromatic and acrid properties in guiding qi downward to resolve upper burner rebellion.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- The response correctly identifies that the patient's symptoms (cold sensation, white greasy coating, desire to vomit) indicate Stomach cold and rebellious qi rather than Liver stagnation.
- The answer explains how Ding Xiang warms the middle burner to resolve the root cold and uses its aromatic nature to guide the upward-rebellious qi downward, unlike methods used for Liver stagnation.

**Reference Content:**
Ding Xiang is a pure yang, acrid, warm, and aromatic herb that enters the Spleen and Stomach channels to warm the middle burner and direct Stomach qi downward. It is essential for treating conditions caused by Stomach cold, such as vomiting, hiccough, and abdominal pain, where stomach qi rises improperly. In clinical practice, it is the core component of the Clove and Persimmon Calyx Decoction, specifically used for hiccough caused by cold from deficiency, as its aromatic nature helps guide the qi downward.

## 2. Lower Burner Yang Deficiency: Uterine Conditions and "Running Piglet"

**Question:**
A female patient reports a history of chronic, persistent uterine bleeding accompanied by a distinct sensation of ice-coldness in the lower abdomen and weakness in the legs. Conversely, another patient presents with a painful, mobile abdominal mass that moves with bowel movements, described in classical texts as "running piglet." How do you apply Ding Xiang to treat the cold congealing blood in the uterus versus the cold stagnation causing the abdominal mass, and what differentiation must be made before prescribing it?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- Ding Xiang's entry into the Kidney channel to warm the lower burner and treat disorders caused by Kidney yang insufficiency.
- The specific application of Ding Xiang for uterine bleeding where cold congeals blood or fluids, indicated by an ice-cold sensation in the lower abdomen.
- The use of Ding Xiang for "running piglet" (mobile abdominal masses) and bulging disorders, provided there is underlying Kidney yang insufficiency and coldness.
- The critical contraindication for Ding Xiang in heat patterns due to its potent warming nature.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- The response correctly links the uterine bleeding and "ice-cold" lower abdomen sensation to the pattern of womb cold from deficiency, making Ding Xiang appropriate when combined with herbs like Shu Di Huang.
- The answer identifies that the "running piglet" condition requires the presence of underlying Kidney yang insufficiency and coldness before Ding Xiang can be used to warm the lower burner and resolve the stagnation.
- The response explicitly states that Ding Xiang is contraindicated if the pattern involves heat, ensuring the practitioner verifies true cold and yang deficiency first.

**Reference Content:**
Beyond the middle burner, Ding Xiang warms the Kidneys and aids the yang, treating conditions like persistent uterine bleeding accompanied by an ice-cold sensation in the lower abdomen, where cold congeals blood or fluids. It is also utilized for lower burner disorders described as "running piglet," painful abdominal masses due to cold, and bulging disorders, provided there is underlying Kidney yang insufficiency and coldness. Clinicians must ensure the pattern involves true cold and yang deficiency, as Ding Xiang's warming nature makes it contraindicated for heat patterns.
