## 1. Differentiating Cold vs. Heat in Acute Vomiting

**Question:**
A patient presents with sudden, violent epigastric pain and retching of clear, unprocessed fluids. Upon further inquiry, they report a strong preference for warmth over cold applications. How do you differentiate this presentation from vomiting caused by Stomach fire, and why is Gao Liang Jiang the appropriate choice here?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- Gao Liang Jiang is indicated for sudden cold turmoil causing epigastric pain, vomiting of clear fluids, and a preference for warmth.
- The herb is contraindicated if nausea stems from Stomach fire or if vomiting is driven by summerheat, as its very warming nature would exacerbate heat patterns.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Correctly identifies the specific pattern of Stomach cold causing rebellious qi and distinguishes it from heat-induced vomiting based on fluid clarity and thermal preferences.
- Demonstrates understanding that Gao Liang Jiang's potency is reserved for severe cold causing "sudden turmoil" rather than mild digestive upset.

**Reference Content:**
Gao Liang Jiang is a potent herb specifically dispersing pathogenic cold within the Spleen and Stomach to stop pain and direct rebellious qi downward. It is the herb of choice for sudden cold pain in the epigastrium, hiccough, and vomiting of clear fluids caused by Stomach cold. However, strict contraindications must be observed; if nausea stems from Stomach fire or sudden turmoil is caused by summerheat, Gao Liang Jiang is inappropriate and potentially harmful.

## 2. Selecting Formulas for Cold-Induced Abdominal Pain

**Question:**
You are treating a patient with cold-induced epigastric pain. How would you modify the formula based on whether the pain is due to simple cold stagnation versus the concurrent stagnation of qi and blood?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- For general cold-induced epigastric pain, Gao Liang Jiang is paired with Cyperi Rhizoma (*Xiang Fu*) as seen in *Liang Fu Wan*.
- If the cold causes stagnation of qi and blood leading to pain, Gao Liang Jiang must be combined with Trogopterori Faeces (*Wu Ling Zhi*).

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Selects the correct herbal pairing based on the specific nature of the pain (qi stagnation vs. qi and blood stagnation).
- Recognizes that while Gao Liang Jiang warms the middle, additional herbs are needed to address the specific type of stagnation present in the channel.

**Reference Content:**
Clinically, specific pairings enhance Gao Liang Jiang's efficacy for distinct presentations. When treating cold-induced epigastric pain, it is paired with Cyperi Rhizoma (*Xiang Fu*), as seen in the formula Galangal and Cyperus Pill (*Liang Fu Wan*). In cases of epigastric pain due to cold causing stagnation of qi and blood, it is combined with Trogopterori Faeces (*Wu Ling Zhi*).
