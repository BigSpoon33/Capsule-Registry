## 1. Differentiating Cold Congealing from Heat Patterns

**Question:**
A patient presents with severe, stabbing pain in the epigastrium and lower abdomen that intensifies upon exposure to cold drafts. They report a desire to wrap their stomach and have no thirst. How would you differentiate this presentation from a similar case of abdominal pain driven by Yin deficiency or Heat in the blood, and why is Bi Ba contraindicated in those specific scenarios?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- The herb is indicated specifically for cold congealing causing qi stagnation in the Stomach and Large Intestine channels.
- Bi Ba is strictly forbidden in patients presenting with Yin deficiency, Heat in the blood, or fever and cough.
- The acrid and hot nature of the herb disperses congealed cold rather than nourishing dryness or clearing heat.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- The response must identify the patient's symptoms (pain worsening with cold, desire to warm) as evidence of cold congealing and qi stagnation.
- The response must explicitly state that using this hot herb on Yin deficiency or heat patterns would exacerbate the condition, citing the specific contraindications from the text.

**Reference Content:**
Bi Ba is indicated for conditions where cold has congealed in the digestive tract, causing obstruction and pain, specifically manifesting as painful obstruction in the epigastrium or abdomen that worsens with cold. However, it is strictly forbidden for patients presenting with Yin deficiency, Heat in the blood level, or fever and cough because its hot nature is not suitable for those who already possess internal heat or dryness.
