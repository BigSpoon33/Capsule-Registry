## Overview
Gan Jiang, also known as dried ginger, represents the older, winter rhizome of the *Zingiberis Rhizoma*. In contrast to the young, tender summer rhizome known as sheng jiang, Gan Jiang is yellowish white, acrid, warm, and drying with a solid texture. These qualities indicate a strong affiliation with the Spleen earth, secondarily affecting the Lungs. It serves as the principal herb for warming the middle burner. Its therapeutic power is distinct; while its ability to disperse is less intense than raw ginger, it is far more prolonged, allowing it to both conserve and mobilize yang energy. Classical texts describe four primary actions: unblocking and assisting Heart yang, expelling stubborn sunken cold within the organs, dispersing cold qi throughout all channels, and treating abdominal pain caused by externally contracted cold.

## Key Concepts
The fundamental nature of Gan Jiang is its capacity to warm the interior and expel cold, addressing both excess conditions from external contraction and deficiency cold resulting from insufficient yang qi. When prepared by frying until black, the herb undergoes a significant transformation known as *Pao Jiang*. In this state, it loses its acridity, becomes bitter, and shifts its nature to be warm and harmonious. This modification changes its primary actions to warming the middle, stopping diarrhea, and arresting bleeding caused by yang qi deficiency.

The herb's relationship with the Kidneys is profound when fried black, allowing it to act at the blood level. A critical theoretical concept involves the dynamic between Gan Jiang and *Aconiti Radix lateralis preparata* (Zhi Fu Zi). Texts assert that Zhi Fu Zi without Gan Jiang is not truly hot, highlighting their synergistic ability to revive primal yang when it is about to be extinguished. Furthermore, Gan Jiang is the most important herb for stopping bleeding when deficient yang fails to contain blood, leading to symptoms like nosebleeds, hematemesis, or bleeding from below. In such scenarios, the herb should be thoroughly dry-fried but not to the point of losing its form.

## Clinical Relevance
Clinically, Gan Jiang is applied to warm the middle and expel cold in the Spleen and Stomach, whether the pathology stems from excess external cold or internal yang deficiency. It effectively dispels wind and dampness that has seeped into the lower burner.

*   **Spleen Deficiency:** It is combined with *Atractylodis macrocephalae Rhizoma* (Bai Zhu) to treat diarrhea associated with Spleen deficiency. Both herbs are often charred to address bloody stools and excessive uterine bleeding.
*   **Severe Yang Deficiency:** When a patient presents with a cold abdomen, vomiting, and diarrhea due to Spleen yang deficiency, Gan Jiang is paired with Ginseng Radix (Ren Shen), as seen in the formula Regulate the Middle Pill (*Li Zhong Wan*).
*   **Debilitated Yang:** For incessant diarrhea resulting from debilitated Spleen and Kidney yang, Gan Jiang is paired with *Aconiti Radix lateralis preparata* (Zhi Fu Zi).
*   **Hemorrhage:** In cases where fire cannot return to its base or deficient yang fails to contain blood, dry-fried Gan Jiang is the key herb for stopping bleeding.

## Summary
Gan Jiang is a robust, warm, and drying herb essential for warming the middle burner and expelling cold. Its unique ability to conserve while mobilizing yang makes it superior to raw ginger for lingering cold patterns. When fried black, it becomes *Pao Jiang*, targeting the blood level, the Kidneys, and various bleeding disorders caused by yang deficiency. Its most potent clinical application involves combining it with Zhi Fu Zi to revive primal yang in life-threatening cold-deficiency states, a partnership so vital that classical texts deem one ineffective without the other.
