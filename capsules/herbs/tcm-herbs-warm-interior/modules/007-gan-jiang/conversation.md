## 1. Differentiating Gan Jiang from Sheng Jiang in Chronic Diarrhea

**Question:**
A patient presents with chronic, watery diarrhea, a cold abdomen, and fatigue, indicating Spleen deficiency and yang deficiency. How would you distinguish whether Gan Jiang or Sheng Jiang is the appropriate choice for warming the middle burner in this specific chronic presentation?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- Gan Jiang is indicated for lingering cold patterns and chronic yang deficiency where the herb's prolonged warming action is superior to raw ginger.
- The transformation of Gan Jiang into Pao Jiang (fried black) specifically addresses severe deficiency cold causing diarrhea and bloody stools, whereas Sheng Jiang lacks this depth of warming power.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- The response must identify Gan Jiang as the superior herb for chronic, lingering cold rather than acute conditions where Sheng Jiang might be used.
- The answer should explain the mechanism of Pao Jiang in stopping diarrhea and arresting bleeding due to yang qi deficiency.

**Reference Content:**
Gan Jiang represents the older, winter rhizome with a solid texture, making it warm and drying with a strong affiliation to the Spleen earth. Its therapeutic power is distinct; while its ability to disperse is less intense than raw ginger, it is far more prolonged, allowing it to both conserve and mobilize yang energy. When prepared by frying until black as Pao Jiang, it loses its acridity and becomes bitter, shifting its nature to warm the middle, stop diarrhea, and arrest bleeding caused by yang qi deficiency.

## 2. Reviving Primal Yang and Stopping Hemorrhage

**Question:**
You are treating a debilitated patient with incessant diarrhea, cold extremities, and sudden bleeding from the nose or gums due to deficient yang failing to contain blood. How do you utilize the specific preparation method of Gan Jiang and its synergistic relationship with Aconite to address this life-threatening pattern?

**Key Concepts to Cover:**
- The synergistic relationship between Gan Jiang and Zhi Fu Zi is critical for reviving primal yang when it is about to be extinguished, as Aconite without Gan Jiang is not truly hot.
- Thoroughly dry-fried Gan Jiang (but not losing its form) is the key herb for stopping hemorrhage when fire cannot return to its base or deficient yang fails to contain blood.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- The response must correctly pair Gan Jiang with Aconiti Radix lateralis preparata (Zhi Fu Zi) for cases of debilitated Spleen and Kidney yang.
- The answer must specify the correct preparation method (thoroughly dry-fried) required for stopping hemorrhage while noting the contraindication of over-frying to the point of losing form.

**Reference Content:**
A critical theoretical concept involves the dynamic between Gan Jiang and Aconiti Radix lateralis preparata (Zhi Fu Zi), with texts asserting that Zhi Fu Zi without Gan Jiang is not truly hot. This combination highlights their synergistic ability to revive primal yang when it is about to be extinguished. Furthermore, Gan Jiang is the most important herb for stopping bleeding when deficient yang fails to contain blood, requiring the herb to be thoroughly dry-fried but not to the point of losing its form.
